Welcome to the Botelho Lab!

The Botelho Lab @ Ryerson University in Toronto, Canada studies the molecular mechanisms that help establish, maintain and change organelle identity and function. Since organelles underpin everything that cells do, then understanding how organelles are made and function is essential to comprehend the fundamentals that drive life, health and disease-states. You are invited to explore and learn about our work and our energetic, fun group. Please contact Dr. Roberto Botelho if interested in graduate or post-doctoral studies.

Laboratory on Organelle Identity and Function

We want to learn how cells build, maintain and change their organelles and what these structures do. Why does this matter? Organelles are the “organs” of the cell. In a very broad sense, organelles compartmentalize and organize myriad biochemical reactions that underpin cell survival and health. Thus, when organelles malfunction, cells become “sick”. This ultimately manifests itself as diseases such as hereditary conditions like Niemann-Pick Disease, infectious diseases like tuberculosis, and can even help turn our cells against our own bodies resulting in cancer or auto- immunity. Thus, by better understanding organelle identity processes, we hope to discover new ways to improve diagnostics and/or therapies to treat diseases. This is a big topic! So, our lab has developed a “soft spot” for specific organelles… the cool ones, that is: lysosomes and phagosomes. Lysosomes are ubiquitous organelles that encase a host of degradative enzymes and an acidic environment. Thus, they are often best known for degrading stuff cells do not want. Hence, they have been often viewed as the “garbage can of the cell” or the “stomach of the cell”. However, if they are a “garbage can” or a “dumb stomach”, then they are a very elegant one. This is because lysosomes are now thought as a command centre for cells, informing them of their metabolic state (are they hungry, full?), stress (are there bugs, damage), and can even signal to make more of themselves or change their shape (hmm.. do garbage cans change and adapt?). So, our lab looks at how cells regulate lysosome function and their properties, especially in macrophages and dendritic cells, which are innate immune cells. Macrophages and dendritic cells are talented cells that do many, many things. But we again work on the “cool things”: phagocytosis and phagosomes. These cells are known as professional phagocytes because they recognize and engulf particles like bacteria, fungi and even dead host cells. This is known as phagocytosis and results in a formally extracellular particle now being sequestered within a new organelle, called the phagosome inside the cell! This poses a potential problem because mammalian cells make for cozy servants. Thankfully, these phagosomes fuse with lysosomes, giving the phagosome enzymes and nasty acid to kill and degrade the would-be naughty bug. But, there’s a bug in this whole process (pun intended). Bacteria like Salmonella, Mycobacteria and Legionella (among many others) can disrupt phagosome maturation to survive within cells. So, phagosomes make for awesome organelles to study organelle biogenesis and identity. Overall, our lab wants to answer questions like these: What makes the lysosome a lysosome? How did a lysosome become a lysosome? How does a lysosome stay as a lysosome? How and for what purpose might a lysosome change? How can phagosomes fuse with lysosomes? How do pathogenic bacteria like Salmonella manipulate phagosome maturation? And so forth….

SELECT RESEARCH PROJECTS

Lysosome remodelling during immune cell challenge

Immune cells like macrophages and dendritic cells have lysosomes that look punctate under the fluorescence microscope. However, once these cells are activated by exposure to bacterial products or cytokines, these lysosomes change! For one thing, we showed that phagocytosis of bacteria enhances expression of lysosomal proteins to enhance lysosomal activity, making macrophages better killers . In other words, phagocytosis trains macrophages to be better killers. For another, lysosomes are transformed from many vesicular structures to a highly tubular network . Though lysosome tubulation may play roles in retaining and processing antigens, this remain poorly understood both in mechanism and in function. We have recently published a review in Cellular Microbiology that summarizes lysosome remodeling and adaptation in immunity.

Phagocytosis and phagosome maturation

Macrophages, neutrophils and dendritic cells are immune cells that hunt down and engulf pathogens with their plasma membrane in a process called phagocytosis. After engulfment, the pathogen is sequestered in a new membrane-bound organelle called the phagosome. Importantly, the phagosome is then converted into a phagolysosome, a process known as phagosome maturation. This transformation changes the phagosome from an innocuous organelle to a very acidic and hydrolytic organelle that kills and digests the pathogen . Studying this system has two great advantages. First, this is a critically important process in the immune arsenal against pathogens – and one that can be hijacked by bugs like Salmonella and Mycobacterium. Second, the phagosome is a beautiful system to study organelle identity processes because we can track an organelle from birth (phagocytosis) to death (phagosome resolution) and study the changes in its identity over many hours. Currently, we are exploring how phosphoinositides regulate phagosome maturation , how cells adapt to phagocytosis and how macrophage handle challenging particles like filamentous Legionella Not yet published, we are also investigating what the heck happens to phagosomes after bacteria are digested!

Phosphatidylinositol-3,5- bisphosphate function and regulation

We are spending buckets of time trying to better understand the regulation and function of phosphatidylinositol-3,5- bisphosphate [PtdIns(3,5)P2], a phosphoinositide that controls properties of lysosomes and the yeast vacuole. Lysosomes and the yeast vacuole are acidic and hydrolytic organelles that degrade and recycle nutrients, damaged or unwanted macromolecules (or entire organelles during autophagy), digest pathogens (see below) and are also important storage organelles. When cells cannot synthesize PtdIns(3,5)P2, the lysosomes become massively swollen, autophagic (link) flux is impaired, phagosome maturation is arrested, even neutrophil chemotaxis is blunted, organisms fail to develop properly, and develop neurological problems, or massive inflammation, etc. So, it kind of matters. For recent work on this see here . We are now trying to understand why lysosomes become massively enlarged and how this lipid coordinates with transcription factors to modulate lysosome biogenesis.
